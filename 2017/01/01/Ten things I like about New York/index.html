What do you think bravery is?
I thought it meant having no fear.
A few weeks ago I went river tracing in Taiwan. I was asked to jump from a huge cliff into a deep lake. My friends jumped in one after another, which looked quite easy. But when it was my turn, I was frightened out, standing still at the edge.
When height and water came to me at the same time, fear doubled. I could not even figure out what I was afraid of at that moment. Although all of my friends were trying to encourage me, I was crying in my heart, feeling vacant-minded and nobody to turn to: why am I supposed to jump from here? Why canâ€™t I just leave? Coward was the last thing I like; and I thought courage meaned no fear.
What a ridiculous mistake.
During my growing up, I seldom went across the road by myself, where my family or my friends around me would always hold my hands. But later on I went to so many places and crossed so many roads, no matter how dangerous or difficult they were, all by myself. It was just an obstacle I was not able to get rid of. Every time I was going across the road, a traffic accident was taking place in my mind. I died a thousand times in this way.
Couple years ago, I took part in a hiking competition in Tibet where we hiked dozens of kilometers in mountains. With the altitude sickness and tireness of long trip, pain was all over on my body and I thought I was going to die. But it was the last moment I would forgive myself. I remebered clearly how my teamamtes encouraged me and pulling me forward. Giving up was easy but it was unfair to them since we were a team.
I did not give up in the end although the medical security asked me to stop. Even though I finished the competition, I was not relieved at all along the time.
I hated that person, who was hindering, not trying the best, thinking of giving up in front of difficulty.
I took this to heart for years. That was why I hiked so frequently and tried to lead others who was doing the same thing like me.
It was never easy. The moment I climbed and cried, that memory came to me again, sitting on the foot of the mountain in Tibet, despairly. Then I bited my silt-and- bruise mixed arm, telling myself to keep grining up, to take the responsibility of being a leader, to encourage my teammates, to guide the strategy of moving farward, to accompany the last one in the team.
